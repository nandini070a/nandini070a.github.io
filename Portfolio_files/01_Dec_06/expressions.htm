  Baichung or Beckham?  

On a very rainy Sunday, 22nd September 2006 at 1500 hours GMT, a bunch of secondees from SPA India to the London office got together for a match of football in London’s Southwark Park.    

The very wet and muddy environment did not deter.  All ventured out into the field not knowing the fate on this soon-to-be eventful day! Team 1 constituted Saumya Bhattacharyya, Abhisek Bhattacharyya, Saumya Ganguli and Sayak Bhanja and Team 2 comprised of Ananya Bhattacharyya, Arijit Ray, Arijit Dey and K V Mahesh.

The match started with a blistering kick by Kolkata’s Abhisek Bhattacharyya which sent the football flying into the air, followed by his right shoe. And there it was…KICK OFF!!!

After resolving the initial confusion of who was in which team, the players scrambled for the ball, sending each other flying off in all possible directions with the ball nowhere to be seen…suddenly a resounding shout.  A goal!!! I  Sayak Bhanja (from Kolkata) had managed to send one past the goalie! (Ananya Bhattacharyya, Kolkata’s Saumya Bhattacharyya’s extremely gallant better half).

However, the joy was short-lived as the afore-mentioned Saumya suffered an injury to his leg and had to be helped off the field. A few minutes of expert medical advice and physio-therapy later, the match resumed.

The tables were soon turned on the leading team who were a man down (Saumya) as Arijit ‘Ronaldinho’ Ray snuck one in past Saumya ‘Kahn’ Ganguli’s watchful eyes, with a move rivalled by few finance professionals. As it was discovered later, it turned out to be Saumya G’s spectacles which were partly at fault. They decided to fall apart after the match. (Those darn glasses!)

It was almost 2-1 as Sayak with the help of a skilful pass from Abhisek, charged towards an empty goal (Ananya-Di, in a very decisive move, had decided to step away!). But an expert tackle from Hyderabad’s Mahesh and a ‘nudge’ from Arijit Dey soon sent the soon-to-be scorer skidding across to his own half. The ‘nudge’ in question is being looked into by the authorities.

The rain finally played spoilsport as it worsened, making visibility down to a few feet. Our champions of the field decided to head (in some cases, limp) off to the cosy interiors of Arijit’s (Ray) residence where all got back some energy over cups of steaming hot tea and everybody’s favourites, cookies and ‘chanachur’. The topic of conversation - Next week’s event!!!

Contributed by:
Sayak Bhanja, SPA, Kolkata

Mein Kampf (My struggle)
By Adolf Hitler

Am I interested in History? Well, the soul search would discreetly remind me of the grades I used to get in school. But then being interested and being an expert need not go hand in hand. Ditto for Politics! Further, considering the general perception about Adolf Hitler, you may question why I chose to read this book, a dictator’s autobiography! Honestly, I craved to know what made people of Germany accept the leadership of Hitler, a dictator! I intended to know the other side of ‘Diary of Anne Frank’.

“Mein Kampf” narrates the journey of Adolf Hitler, a painter from a middle class family to a politician. Though it glorifies his views and tries to justify why Jews are not “German” and hence not worthy of living a decent peaceful life, it was very difficult for me to relate to such thinking and brutality. The political situation described by him recounts the feeling that Germans were being sidelined. He also maintains that Jews were the hindrance in the progress of Germans, though the exact details for the same are not sufficiently illustrated.

However strange it may seem, I could relate to the situation, which forced Adolf Hitler and the people around him to behave the way they did. Each person has a different way of reacting to a complicated situation. Some people may resign to fate and some may try to rebel. Rebels cannot be brandished as evil, but the way in which the rebels carry out their propaganda may have to be carefully looked at.

I admire that at the age when we are not even sure of which career to choose, Adolf Hitler lost his parents, started to live life struggling on his own and still had the frame of mind to analyse the political situation. The leadership qualities, the oratory skills were what enabled him grasp the nerves of common man. Such was his proficiency over these skills, that he eventually changed destiny of people worldwide!

While reading the book, it seems that the situation in Germany then may not be as unpleasant as we currently have in our country. However, the culture, upbringing and tolerance level may differ to quite an extent. Currently, in India, the fact that we do not have such rebels may not work in our favour. But if not for this fact, there would have definitely been another world war, which would have been in nobody’s favour!

To sum up, this book is a classic indeed, never to be neglected, especially, if you don’t want to see Hitlers rising to power in our nation.

Contributed by:
Madhura Umrani, Pune

  My First ….

My first skydiving experience

After surviving at Amsterdam where more than 1700 tax professionals participated from over 60 countries, I decided to take up one of the adventure sports. Switzerland offers several options for this. I had done paragliding at Manali and hence decided to go one step ahead and that was skydiving - to jump from an airplane.

Before boarding the airplane with my wife, two Americans working in UK with Citigroup and two Korean who were on a vacation, we were given instructions by the instructors. My question on number of people dying every year as a result of sky diving was answered in zero. However, it was said, that there were instances of some trackers dying every year as they lose path.  All along I was under the impression that tracking was the safest thing to do.

The thrill of this adventure started during the flight to the drop zone, as we had ample time to enjoy the wonderful scenery of the mighty Swiss Alps. I thought I had fully recovered the price I paid, but the best was yet to come. At 13,000 ft (4000 m) it was exit time and they opened the door of the airplane. For all these years I was under the impression that at such height and speed, when the door of the airplane is opened, the plane itself may get damaged/blown up or the pilot may lose control over the craft. Had seen our (Shahrukh does this in Don) and Hollywood heros doing stunts, but not always what is shown in movies is true. The things inside the craft were well settled even after opening the door. My wife was first to jump out of the airplane and they smartly did not give me enough time to think as to what I was going to do. I jumped in quick succession (quicker than our batsman who return to pavilion one after another) with a qualified jumper strapped to my back.

The next 45 to 60 seconds gave me the most thrilling experience of my life. This was ‘free fall’ from 13000ft in Swiss Alps. You may think that 45 seconds is too short to experience anything but believe me the person who jumps for the first time finds 45 seconds to be extremely long as he thinks whether this was the last jump of his life. The free fall is enough to take one's breath away as you plunge down the vertical face into the seemingly bottomless valleys of the Swiss Alps. These were the most exiting moments with the bonus of a really great flight before you even get to the jump site. With your pulse rate going through the roof and the wind screaming in your ears you have 45 seconds of heart stopping free fall in a natural setting, even more impressive than the jump itself. I could never understand when I achieved the horizontal position, as when I jumped I was in vertical position. May be there was something in the instructions given to me or may be some basic scientific principle. After some moments I realised that there was one more person (other than the jumper strapped to my back) flying/falling with me and was requesting me to smile. That was the cameraman. To my surprise at such height, speed with gravity at its best he was efficiently clicking photos and shooting the entire event.  He managed to click more than 60 photos in the flying/falling position in the sky from different directions. Probably this makes me change my opinion, and everything they show in the movies need not be fake.

After the free fall the jumpmaster inflated the parachute and things settled down remarkably. The parachute opened up and the speed reduced considerably in the next few minutes, which were equally good, as I was hanging in the sky and enjoying the scenic beauty of Switzerland from a great height.

Contributed by:
 Radhakishan Rawal, TRS, Mumbai

Q. How long have you been with PwC?
Ans: Forever!  Started my career with PwC as an article trainee in 1985. I took a three year break when I had my daughter and resumed when she was three. It’s been 18 long years now.
Q. How does a normal working day pass?
Ans: My days at office are filled with phone calls, reviews, discussions, debates and sometimes arguments. It also includes discussing project submissions required in school, dinner menu, exams and studies with my daughter Sanjana.
Q. How do you spend your weekends, holidays?
Ans: I love reading books - mainly fiction, shopping, relaxing, and spending time with Sanjana, as there is so much to catch up on.
Q. Your favorite destination?
Ans:I like to go someplace new every holiday and have loved every place I have visited.  Any place which has lots of greenery, peace and natural beauty is great for a holiday.
Q. Please share your hobbies and how do you pursue them?
Ans: I love reading and make sure that I read every night.  I love to potter around the house and spend weekends doing that. I am also a firm believer in the retail therapy, so ensure that I get some shopping done.
Q. What are the kind of job demands and family commitments, you have  faced?
Ans: This is a daily struggle that all working ladies have to deal with.  I strongly believe that one must be able to strike a good balance between personal and professional life. I remember getting files at home at night and staying up crazy hours to clear them so that client deadlines would not be missed. I remember on one occasion bringing Sanjana to the office when she was suffering form malaria, as we had to complete the work for an IPO.  But I also remember my colleagues helping out when I had to be at home for my dad-in-law's surgery.
Q. What does your family think of your work and PwC?
Ans: I have never worked anywhere else so cannot make any comparisons. I love this place and being a part of it.  My family shares my views, though seeing my routine my daughter is sure she does not want to be a C.A.  Abhay, at times, does get irritated when I cannot attend all the naval functions, but he is supportive.
Q. How and with whom do you share household responsibilities?
Ans: Unlike the modern liberated woman I must say that the house and the related responsibilities is completely my domain – I however make sure that I have great support system to take care of the details.
Q. How supportive were your colleagues, seniors in special times of need?
Ans: Very supportive!!!. I have never faced a problem ever.
Q. Words of wisdom that you would like to share with all.
Ans: You have only one life so make sure you enjoy every moment.  Also - especially the men, work and home are equally important so do not sacrifice one for the other.
